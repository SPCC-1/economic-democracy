250 Voices — Core Anthology

Paired resource This guide accompanies: 250 Voices — Core Anthology (Free on the website)

The anthology is not a reading assignment. It is a map. Two hundred and fifty figures — one for every year of the republic — organized into eight sections that hold the full tension of how power and economics have been argued, organized, theorized, and contested in American life. The teacher's job is not to assign the whole thing. It is to help students navigate it strategically: to find the figures who illuminate a problem, to place them on the matrix, and to hear the argument that runs between them.

The organizing principle of the anthology is deliberate tension. Hamilton and Jefferson sit in the same section. Martin Luther King Jr. and Steve Bannon are in Section VII. adrienne maree brown and Marc Andreessen are in Section IV. Louis Kelso and Milton Friedman are in Section V. The point is not that these figures are equivalent — it is that they are responding to the same structural conditions and reaching different conclusions. Students cannot understand the argument without hearing both sides of it.

Core teacher framing: "This is not a list of heroes. It is not a list of villains. It is a map of the argument the United States has been having with itself for 250 years — about who should hold economic power, and who it should answer to. Your job is to find where you are on this map. And then to find the person who disagrees with you most directly — and understand why they are wrong, or why they might be right."
IWhat the Anthology Teaches

The anthology is a tool, not a syllabus. Used well, it builds the following capacities over time:

  • Recognize that every major economic argument has a lineage — it did not begin with the current political moment
  • Place a specific figure on the Power–Democracy–Economy matrix and defend the placement with evidence
  • Identify the figure whose argument most directly challenges their own default position — and engage it seriously
  • Understand why intelligent, informed people in the same section reach opposing conclusions
  • Connect a historical figure to a contemporary debate — and identify what has changed and what has not
  • Recognize that the storytellers (Section VIII) are economic actors too — narrative shapes what is considered possible
IISection-by-Section Teacher Guide

Each of the eight sections has a core argument and a set of internal tensions. Notes for each section follow — including the central disagreement, suggested pairings, and best entry points for classroom use.

IArchitects of the Experiment

The founders built a republic designed to prevent tyranny — but disagreed from the first Congress about what tyranny looked like. That argument has never ended. This section includes the architects of the original experiment alongside the abolitionists, suffragists, and labor organizers who spent the next century demanding it deliver on its promise.

Hamilton (concentrated federal power) vs. Jefferson (decentralized agrarian independence) Franklin (self-made myth) vs. Paine (radical redistribution) Founders' promises vs. Douglass / Tubman / Truth on their incompleteness Lincoln's free labor ideology vs. the economic reality of Reconstruction's failure

Best entry point: Hamilton vs. Jefferson — still the clearest statement of the central argument. Best matrix exercise: place Paine (Q I) and Madison (Q II/III border) and ask why they were in the same room.

IIIdeas That Built the World

The economists, philosophers, and system-builders whose ideas became the operating system of modern capitalism — and their challengers. Marx is here not as a political figure but as an analyst of industrial capitalism whose diagnosis has outlasted his prescription.

Smith (self-regulating markets) vs. Marx (capital as accumulated labor) Keynes (managed demand) vs. Hayek (spontaneous order) Friedman (markets as democracy) vs. Ostrom (commons without markets) Kelso (universal capital ownership) vs. the wage-only model all others assume

Best entry point: Keynes vs. Hayek — the clearest statement of the Q I/Q II divide. Best surprise figure: Kelso — most students have never heard of him, but his argument (everyone should own capital, not just earn wages) is the intellectual ancestor of the curriculum's central thesis.

IIIScale, Labor & the Machine

What happens when markets scale beyond what any individual can see, understand, or contest? This section covers industrialization, labor organizing, the trust era, and the first great wave of democratic response to concentrated economic power.

Carnegie / Rockefeller (concentration as efficiency) vs. Debs / Goldman (labor as commons) Teddy Roosevelt (regulated capitalism) vs. Taft / laissez-faire tradition Ford (high wages as consumer strategy) vs. Taylor (scientific management of labor) Jane Addams (settlement house economics) vs. industrial philanthropy as power preservation

Best entry point: Carnegie and Debs in the same room — they are responding to identical conditions and reaching opposite conclusions about who the economy is for. Best matrix exercise: place Carnegie (Q IV) and Debs (Q I) and trace the arc from one to the other through Roosevelt (Q II).

IVThe Digital Revolution & Its Critics

The platform economy is the newest incarnation of the oldest argument: when a technology scales fast enough to restructure entire economies, who governs it — and how? This section places the builders alongside the critics who saw what was being built before most people noticed.

Andreessen / Thiel (private power, elite control) vs. Gebru / Zuboff (accountability and harm) Lanier (data ownership) vs. Zuckerberg (data as platform asset) Altman (AI as abundance) vs. critics of AI concentration Wu (new Brandeis antitrust) vs. platform monopoly as natural efficiency

Best entry point: Thiel's "competition is for losers" vs. Khan's antitrust revival — the starkest Q IV vs. Q I argument in contemporary economics. Best for student self-placement: ask students where they sit on this section's spectrum before and after reading. Most shift.

VMoney, Wealth & Who Controls It

Capital allocation is not a neutral technical process — it is a political act. This section maps the full range of arguments about how wealth is created, held, and distributed: from behavioral economics to sovereign wealth, from shareholder primacy to stakeholder capitalism.

Friedman (shareholder primacy) vs. Freeman (stakeholder theory) Buffett (patient long-term capital) vs. activist shareholder extractivism Mazzucato (public investment as innovation engine) vs. private venture model Piketty (r > g as structural law) vs. growth-optimist responses

Best entry point: Piketty's r > g argument — accessible, structural, and immediately connects to the framework's central question about who holds economic power. Best surprise: Mazzucato — students who believe in markets are often startled to learn how much of Silicon Valley was built on public funding.

VIPower, Accountability & Democratic Renewal

What does it take to make economic power answerable to democratic life? This section covers the full range of responses — from regulatory reform to worker organizing, from climate economics to global solidarity movements.

Reich / McGhee (solidarity economics) vs. zero-sum racial framing Klein (shock doctrine) vs. technocratic crisis management Kelley (radical Black imagination) vs. pragmatic incremental reform Global south voices (Shiva, Maathai, Mandela) vs. Northern economic frameworks

Best entry point: Heather McGhee's "solidarity dividend" — the argument that racial division costs everyone, including white working-class voters, measurably. Best cross-section pairing: McGhee (VI) and Arlie Hochschild (VII) — two accounts of the same white working-class economic anxiety reaching opposite conclusions about its cause.

VIIEconomic Anger, Identity & Mobilization

Economic anxiety is among the most politically volatile forces in any democracy. This section maps the full range of how economic anger becomes political mobilization — from radical labor to Black liberation, from socialist left to nationalist right. King and Bannon are in the same section because they are responding to the same economic reality and reaching opposite conclusions.

King (Poor People's Campaign, democratic socialism) vs. Trump/Bannon (nationalist economics) Malcolm X (Black economic self-determination) vs. Sowell/Steele (individual agency) Hampton (class solidarity across race) vs. racial identity as primary organizing frame AOC (democratic intervention) vs. Buchanan/Perot (populism from the right)

Best entry point: King and Bannon — the juxtaposition is deliberately provocative. Both are responding to economic exclusion; their diagnoses and prescriptions are opposite. This pairing reliably generates the most productive student disagreement. Handle carefully: the goal is analysis, not equivalence.

VIIIWho Tells the Story of the Economy

Before people can change an economy, they need to be able to see it. This section belongs to the writers, poets, journalists, satirists, and public intellectuals who shaped how Americans understand power and money. The Nuyorican oral tradition of the South Bronx belongs in this canon not as a footnote but as one of its clearest economic witnesses.

Baldwin / Morrison / Coates (structural witness) vs. Kiyosaki / Gladwell (individual narrative) Pedro Pietri / Piri Thomas (inside the economic margin) vs. mainstream economic journalism Rogan / Carlson / Shapiro (attention economy as political economy) vs. accountability journalism Le Guin / Jemisin (speculative economic imagination) vs. realist economic analysis

Best entry point: Pedro Pietri's "Puerto Rican Obituary" — a funeral for the American Dream from inside the South Bronx. Most students have not encountered this voice. It reframes the economic argument from inside experience rather than outside analysis. Best question: "Who gets to be an economic thinker? What counts as economic argument?" Section VIII is the answer.

IIIFour Lesson Models

The anthology supports multiple lesson formats. Four tested models follow — from a single-period placement exercise to a multi-day research project.

Model A

The Cold Open

15–20 min · Any point in the curriculum

Display 6–8 names from one section. No context. Ask students: "Place these figures on the matrix based only on what you know or can infer." Reveal the section intro. Discuss which placements held — and why students made the assumptions they did about unfamiliar names.

Model B

The Deliberate Pairing

45–60 min · Section-specific

Assign two figures from the same section who sit in opposing quadrants. Students research both (one class period or homework), then conduct a structured debate: each student argues from their assigned figure's position. Debrief with matrix placement and "what would it take for one to persuade the other?"

Model C

The Self-Placement

30–40 min · After framework lesson

Students choose three figures from anywhere in the anthology: one whose argument they find most compelling, one whose argument most challenges them, and one they had never heard of. They place all three on the matrix and write a paragraph on what the three together reveal about where they themselves stand.

Model D

The Research Profile

2–3 class periods · Unit project

Students select one figure from the anthology for independent research. Deliverable: a one-page profile in the same format as the 28 in-depth profiles — context, core argument, supporter and critic perspectives, quadrant placement, and one open question. This is the student contribution to an expanding class anthology.

IVHigh-Yield Cross-Section Pairings

The most generative pairings are often across sections — figures who are responding to the same structural problem from different historical moments or ideological positions. These pairings work reliably for structured debate, think-pair-share, or written analysis.

Hamilton (I) Andreessen (IV) Both argue for concentrated power as engine of national strength — 230 years apart. What has changed? What hasn't?
Debs (III) Chris Smalls (VII) Union organizing across a century of industrial change. Same argument, different economy. What did the movement win? What did it lose?
Keynes (II) Mazzucato (V) Both argue that markets require public investment to function. Mazzucato takes Keynes's logic into the innovation economy. How does the argument evolve?
King (VII) Hochschild (VII) Two accounts of economic anger in America — one from the Black freedom movement, one from the white rural working class. What do they share? Where do they diverge?
Ostrom (II) Lanier (IV) Both argue for non-market governance of shared resources — Ostrom for physical commons, Lanier for data. What would a Lanier-Ostrom framework for the internet actually look like?
Pietri / Thomas (VIII) Ehrenreich (VI) Poverty from inside (South Bronx oral tradition) vs. poverty documented from outside (journalism). What does each mode of witness make visible that the other misses?
Friedman M. (II) McGhee (VI) Friedman: markets are color-blind and self-correcting. McGhee: racial division costs everyone measurably. They cannot both be right. Who has the better evidence?
VHandling Difficult Figures

Several figures in the anthology are politically charged in ways that can shut down discussion if not handled carefully. Notes on the most common challenges:

Section VII

Trump, Bannon, and the Nationalist Right

These figures belong in the anthology because they are responding to real economic conditions — deindustrialization, wage stagnation, the perceived abandonment of the white working class. The question is not whether their diagnosis is correct. The question is: what economic argument are they making, and where does it sit on the matrix?

Teacher move: "Set aside whether you agree or disagree with this figure politically. What is the economic argument they are making? Who does it benefit? Who does it harm? Where does it sit on the Power–Democracy–Economy matrix?" This keeps the analysis structural rather than personal.

Section VII

Malcolm X, Assata Shakur, Huey Newton

These figures are sometimes treated as too radical for classroom use. They are in the anthology because their economic arguments — about self-determination, land, capital, and the cost of survival in an extractive state — are rigorous and historically grounded. The same analytical frame applies: what is the economic argument? Where does it sit on the matrix?

Teacher move: "Frederick Douglass and Malcolm X are both in Section I/VII. Both are responding to economic exclusion. What is different about their proposed responses — and what does the difference tell us about what changed between 1865 and 1965?"

Section VIII

Rogan, Carlson, Shapiro — Media Figures as Economic Actors

These figures are in Section VIII not as political endorsements but as examples of how the attention economy shapes economic argument. The question is structural: what economic argument does each figure's platform amplify? Who does their audience become, economically, after consuming their content?

Teacher move: "Joe Rogan has more listeners than any cable news network. That is an economic fact. What economic arguments does his platform make visible — and which does it make invisible? That is the Section VIII question for every figure in this section."

VIDifferentiation Options

Entry level

Single Section Focus

Assign one section only. Use the Cold Open model (Model A). Students place 6–8 figures from that section on the matrix. No prior knowledge required — inference and curiosity are the skills.

Standard

Paired Figure Analysis

Assign one cross-section pairing from Section IV of this guide. Students research both figures and complete a structured written analysis. One class period of research plus one of discussion.

Advanced

Original Profile Research

Model D — students produce an original profile of any anthology figure. Deliverable matches the format of the 28 in-depth profiles. Contributes to a class-built expansion of the anthology itself.

VIIAssessment Guidance

What to assess

Map literacy

Can the student navigate the anthology as a map — identifying where figures sit relative to each other, not just listing what they believed?

Tension recognition

Does the student identify the genuine intellectual tension between two figures — not just describe them separately? Can they say what is actually at stake in the disagreement?

Matrix placement

Can the student place a figure on the Power–Democracy–Economy matrix with a defensible argument — including acknowledging why an alternative placement is also possible?

Engagement with difficulty

Does the student engage seriously with figures they disagree with — or do they dismiss them? The goal is analytical capacity, not ideological alignment.

Historical grounding

Does the student connect a figure's argument to the historical conditions that produced it? Economic arguments do not arise in a vacuum — the conditions matter.

"This is not a canon. It is a map of how power argues with itself.

The map is only useful if it shows the whole terrain.
Your job is not to find the right side.
Your job is to understand the argument well enough
to participate in it responsibly."
