Civic Economy Role-Play Lab

The Welfare State, Demographic Change, and Intergenerational Responsibility

Working World LLC  ·  Alfredo Mathew III  ·  Grades 11–12  ·  90–120 minutes

Essential Question

How should a shrinking workforce support an aging society — fairly, sustainably, and democratically?

IWhy This Issue Matters

For most of U.S. history, the welfare state was built on a simple assumption: many workers supporting fewer retirees. That assumption is breaking down — and the pressures are structural, not ideological.

  • People are living longer
  • Birth rates are falling
  • Fewer workers paying payroll taxes
  • Healthcare costs are rising
  • Debt levels are already high
  • Social Security and Medicare remain among the most popular programs in American history

This creates a true governing dilemma — not an ideological one. There are no painless solutions. Only tradeoffs. Students will be asked to find them.

IIHistorical Precedents to Study

Before beginning the role-play, research and be prepared to reference at least two of the following:

  • Creation of Social Security (1930s)
  • Expansion of Medicare and Medicaid (1960s)
  • Payroll tax funding model
  • Postwar demographic boom
  • Past benefit adjustments (retirement age, COLAs)
  • Use of debt to fund social programs

Key historical insight: The welfare state was created through reform, not revolution — and has been repeatedly adjusted rather than abandoned.

IIIThe Governing Problem

You are part of a national commission tasked with keeping Social Security and Medicare viable for the next 75 years.

You must decide: Who pays? How much? For how long? And who bears the cost of transition? Your proposal must be financially plausible, politically defensible, acknowledge intergenerational fairness, and draw on historical precedent.

IVRole Archetypes — Assign 5–7

Each role has legitimate interests. No one is "wrong." Your job is to represent your role honestly — then negotiate across differences.

Role 1

Retired Worker

Paid into the system for decades. Depends on benefits for basic security. Feels promises should be honored.

Core concern

"I did what was asked of me. Don't change the rules now."

Role 2

Young Worker / Student

Faces student debt, housing costs, job insecurity. Doubts the system will exist when they retire.

Core concern

"Why should I pay more into a system I may never receive?"

Role 3

Small Business Owner

Pays payroll taxes. Worried about labor costs and competitiveness. Weighs employee welfare against survival.

Core concern

"Higher taxes could mean fewer jobs or lower wages."

Role 4

Wealth Holder / Investor

Income comes from capital, not wages. Often pays less into payroll tax-based systems. Questions the targeting of wealth.

Core concern

"Why should wealth be targeted instead of fixing spending?"

Role 5

Public Finance Economist

Focuses on long-term sustainability. Warns against unlimited debt. Treats the math as non-negotiable.

Core concern

"Promises must match resources, or the system collapses."

Role 6

Technology Executive

Operates in AI and automation-driven economy. Productivity rising, labor share shrinking. Questions whether old funding models still apply.

Core concern

"If technology replaces labor, funding models must change."

Role 7

Elected Lawmaker

Balances voters, budgets, and legitimacy. Knows reforms are politically risky. Wants to survive the next election.

Core concern

"What can actually pass without destroying trust?"

Note

Facilitation tip

Roles can be combined or split depending on class size. The goal is that every position at the table has a genuine advocate. Avoid assigning roles that match students' own stated positions — productive discomfort is part of the learning.

VPolicy Options on the Table

Groups must evaluate at least three options. Combinations are encouraged.

1 Raise Payroll Taxes Maintains existing structure. Politically familiar. Burdens workers directly. May reduce employment.
2 Wealth or Capital Gains Tax Shifts burden upward. Addresses inequality. Raises questions about incentives and enforcement.
3 Technology / Data / AI Tax Reflects automation replacing labor. Forward-looking. Difficult to define and administer. Precedent unclear.
4 Reduce or Means-Test Benefits Protects long-term finances. Targets need. Risks elderly poverty. May break political trust.
5 Raise Retirement Age Reflects longer lifespans. Reduces liability. Unequal impact: physical laborers cannot wait longer.
6 Fund with Debt Preserves benefits now. Avoids immediate political pain. Shifts burden to future generations. Unsustainable long-term.
VICore Student Task
  1. Propose a reform package. No "do nothing" or "abolish everything" options. Every proposal must be financially plausible and include at least two policy options from the table above.
  2. Explain tradeoffs clearly. Who pays more? Who pays less? Who is protected? Who takes on risk? Write it out explicitly before presenting.
  3. Defend with historical precedent. How has the U.S. adjusted social programs before? Why does reform — not rupture — make sense here?
  4. Negotiation phase. Form coalitions across roles. Concede something important. Preserve system legitimacy. You must find at least one other role to partner with.
VIIReflection — Back to the Matrix

After the role-play, step out of your role and answer the following:

Where does your group's proposal sit on the Power–Democracy–Economy matrix? Which quadrant — and why?

Is your proposal closer to reform, revolution, or counterrevolution? Explain using the framework from the Revolution/Counterrevolution/Reform reading.

Whose trust does your proposal preserve — and whose does it risk losing? Why does that matter for a democracy?

Democracy is not choosing between good and bad options.
It is choosing among difficult options without breaking the system.
