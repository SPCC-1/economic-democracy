Section I · Architects of the Experiment

Mother Jones

Organizing, Solidarity, and the Moral Economy of Labor

Suggested Quadrant: Q I 1837–1930 Labor Organizer & Activist

To understand Mary Harris Jones, you first have to understand organization—and why power in the economy is not only held, but built through collective action over time.

By the late 19th century, industrial capitalism had transformed the structure of work. Large corporations controlled production, and workers—often immigrants, women, and children—labored under harsh conditions for low wages, with little security or voice. The question was no longer whether inequality existed, but how those affected by it could respond within systems that offered them limited leverage.

Mother Jones’s answer is grounded in practice rather than theory.

At the center of her worldview is a clear and forceful claim:

Workers gain power not individually, but collectively—through organization, persistence, and shared struggle.

Jones is not a policy designer or an academic thinker. She is an organizer, moving from one industrial site to another—mines, factories, rail yards—building relationships, mobilizing workers, and sustaining movements under difficult conditions. Her work focuses on creating solidarity among individuals who might otherwise remain isolated and vulnerable.

This is not an abstract concept.

Solidarity, for Jones, is operational. It means coordinating strikes, supporting families during work stoppages, building networks that can withstand employer pressure, and maintaining commitment over time.

Through these actions, workers begin to shift the balance of power, even within systems that remain structurally unequal.

From this perspective, Jones introduces a practical dimension to the broader argument: Debs articulates the possibility of collective ownership. Gompers focuses on negotiation within the system. Jones demonstrates how power is actually built on the ground—through sustained organizing.

Her work highlights the importance of movement-building as a form of economic intervention. Change does not occur solely through policy or institutional design; it also emerges from the capacity of individuals to act together in pursuit of shared goals.

Perspective Supporters

Supporters see Mother Jones as a central figure in the development of labor power.

They argue that she understood something essential about the dynamics of industrial economies: that without organization, workers remain fragmented and unable to influence outcomes. By creating structures of solidarity, she enabled workers to challenge existing conditions and push for improvements in wages, safety, and dignity.

From this perspective, her work contributes to a broader understanding of economic democracy as a process:

Power is not given. It is built through collective effort.

Perspective Critics

Critics, however, raise questions about the limits of organizing as a strategy.

They argue that while collective action can produce gains, it often operates within existing structures rather than transforming them. Improvements in conditions may be temporary or uneven, and gains can be reversed when economic or political conditions change. This raises questions about how organizing connects to longer-term structural change.

A deeper critique examines the sustainability of solidarity.

Maintaining collective action over time requires resources, coordination, and shared commitment. Differences among workers—across industries, regions, and identities—can make sustained unity difficult. This highlights the challenge of building coalitions that are both broad and durable.

Mother Jones did not design the economic system, nor did she propose a comprehensive alternative. But she showed how individuals within that system can act collectively to reshape their conditions.

Her legacy raises enduring questions: How do people build power when they start with very little? What forms of organization are necessary to sustain collective action? And how can movements translate immediate gains into lasting change?

These questions deepen the argument you are exploring. They shift the focus from structure and policy to practice and participation—to the ways in which people, acting together, can influence the systems that shape their lives. And they remind us that economic democracy is not only a matter of design, but of ongoing collective effort.
