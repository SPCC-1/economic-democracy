Section IV · The Digital Revolution & Its Critics

Mark Zuckerberg

Social Networks, Data, and the Monetization of Attention

Suggested Quadrant: IV 1984–present

To understand Mark Zuckerberg, you have to begin with a coordination question: what happens when human relationships themselves become infrastructure?

In the early 21st century, the internet evolved from static pages to dynamic networks of people. The key shift was not just access to information, but the ability to map, connect, and engage billions of users in real time.

Zuckerberg built for that system.

At the center of his worldview is a defining claim:

Connecting people at scale creates powerful networks that can be organized, analyzed, and monetized.

As the co-founder of Facebook (now Meta), Zuckerberg developed platforms that structure social interaction — news feeds, messaging systems, groups, and identity layers. These systems transformed communication into continuous, data-generating activity.

From this perspective, attention is a resource. User engagement — likes, shares, comments, time spent — becomes measurable and monetizable. Advertising systems are then optimized to target users based on behavior, preferences, and network connections.

This created a new form of power:

Control over the architecture of social interaction and the data it produces.

Platforms like Facebook and Instagram do not just host content — they shape what people see, how information spreads, and how communities form. Algorithms determine visibility, influencing both individual experience and public discourse.

This reflects a broader framework: Digital platforms can transform social life into economic value.

Perspective Supporters

Supporters see Zuckerberg as a builder of global connectivity.

They argue that social platforms have enabled communication across distance, supported community formation, and provided tools for organizing, expression, and entrepreneurship. Billions of people gained the ability to connect and share information.

From this perspective, Zuckerberg expands the analysis of economic systems to include attention, data, and networks as core assets.

Perspective Critics

Critics, however, raise significant concerns.

They argue that the monetization of attention can incentivize content that maximizes engagement rather than accuracy or well-being. Issues related to misinformation, polarization, and mental health have been widely debated.

Critics also point to structural risks: when a single platform shapes the flow of information, it can influence democratic processes and public perception.

A deeper tension lies in the relationship between connection and control. Systems designed to connect people can also centralize authority over communication. Who decides what is amplified, suppressed, or monetized?

Zuckerberg’s expansion into virtual and augmented reality reflects an effort to extend this model — building not just platforms for interaction, but environments where interaction occurs.

Mark Zuckerberg did not invent social connection. But he transformed it into a scalable, data-driven platform — demonstrating how networks of people can become the foundation of economic and social power.

His legacy raises enduring questions: Who controls the platforms that mediate human connection? How should attention and data be governed? And what are the societal implications of monetizing social interaction at scale?
