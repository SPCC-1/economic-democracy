Section VII · Economic Anger, Identity & Mobilization

Leonard Peltier

Indigenous Sovereignty, State Power, and the Struggle for Justice

Suggested Quadrant: I 1944–present Indigenous Rights Activist

To understand Leonard Peltier, you have to begin with a sovereignty question: what happens when Indigenous nations exist within a legal and political system that does not fully recognize their autonomy?

Peltier’s life and case are rooted in the broader history of Native American displacement, treaty violations, and conflict between Indigenous communities and the U.S. government. As a member of the American Indian Movement (AIM), his activism emerged from efforts to assert Indigenous rights and sovereignty.

At the center of his worldview is a defining claim:

The U.S. legal and political system has historically failed to deliver justice for Indigenous peoples.

He argues that Indigenous communities have faced systemic marginalization—through land dispossession, economic exclusion, and legal frameworks that limit self-determination. From this perspective, justice is inseparable from sovereignty.

Peltier was convicted in connection with the 1975 shooting deaths of two FBI agents on the Pine Ridge Reservation. His trial and imprisonment have become a focal point of international debate.

Beyond the specifics of the case, Peltier’s writings and advocacy emphasize Indigenous self-determination, cultural survival, and resistance to external control. He frames his experience within a longer history of conflict between Indigenous nations and federal authority.

This reflects a broader framework:

Colonial legacies continue to shape contemporary economic and political conditions.

His work highlights issues such as land rights, resource extraction, and the economic marginalization of Indigenous communities—linking these to questions of sovereignty and governance. Economic and political autonomy, in this view, are interconnected.

Perspective Supporters

Supporters see Peltier as a symbol of resistance.

They argue that his conviction was flawed—citing issues such as withheld evidence, questionable testimony, and procedural irregularities. They view him as a political prisoner and a symbol of broader injustices faced by Indigenous activists. His case brings attention to systemic inequities and the need for reform in how Indigenous rights are recognized and protected.

Perspective Critics

Critics emphasize accountability and the rule of law.

They maintain that the conviction was justified and emphasize the seriousness of the crimes involved. They argue that legal processes were followed and that the case should not be reframed as political persecution. Political framing, they contend, should not override legal outcomes or the gravity of violent acts.

A deeper tension lies in legitimacy. Who has the authority to define justice in contexts shaped by historical dispossession and contested sovereignty? Peltier’s legacy does not resolve this question. Instead, it highlights it.

Leonard Peltier represents a deeply contested figure in American political and legal history: one whose life and case sit at the intersection of Indigenous rights, state power, and the meaning of justice.

What does sovereignty mean in practice for Indigenous nations? How should legal systems address historical injustice? And how can accountability and fairness be balanced in deeply contested cases?
