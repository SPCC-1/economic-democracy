Section IV · The Digital Revolution & Its Critics

Kate Crawford

Artificial Intelligence, Power, and the Material Foundations of Technology

Suggested Quadrant: I Present AI Researcher & Scholar

To understand Kate Crawford, you have to begin with a systems question: what is artificial intelligence actually made of?

AI is often described as abstract—algorithms, models, and data. But Crawford redirects attention to the physical and social systems that make these technologies possible.

At the center of her worldview is a defining claim:

Artificial intelligence is built on material resources, labor, and power structures that are often hidden from view.

She argues that AI systems rely on vast supply chains—mining for rare earth minerals, energy-intensive data centers, and large-scale human labor for data labeling and content moderation.

From this perspective, AI is not immaterial. It is embedded in global systems of extraction, production, and governance. The environmental and human costs of these systems are part of the technology itself. This creates a distinct form of power:

Control over the infrastructure and resources that enable AI.

Crawford also examines how AI systems reflect social dynamics. Data sets are shaped by historical and cultural contexts, which can introduce bias into models. Algorithmic systems can reproduce or amplify existing inequalities if these dynamics are not addressed.

This reflects a broader framework: technological systems are inseparable from the social and political environments in which they are built.

Perspective Supporters

Supporters see Crawford as a critical analyst of AI.

They argue that her work expands the conversation beyond technical performance to include ethics, labor, environment, and governance. By making these hidden systems visible, she broadens the understanding of what AI entails.

From this perspective, Crawford expands the analysis of economic systems to include the full lifecycle of technology—from resource extraction to deployment.

Perspective Critics

Critics, however, raise counterpoints.

Some argue that while these issues are real, they must be balanced against the benefits of AI, including efficiency, innovation, and new capabilities across sectors.

Others suggest that focusing on structural critiques may not always provide clear pathways for implementation or reform.

A deeper tension lies in the relationship between innovation and responsibility. How can societies continue to develop advanced technologies while accounting for their environmental, labor, and social impacts? What standards should govern these systems?

Crawford’s work emphasizes accountability. She calls for greater transparency, regulation, and public engagement in the development and deployment of AI systems.

Kate Crawford does not build AI systems at scale. But she reframes how they are understood—demonstrating that technology is not just code, but a complex system of resources, labor, and power.

What are the true costs of artificial intelligence? Who bears those costs, and who benefits? And how should societies govern technologies that are embedded in global systems of extraction and inequality?
