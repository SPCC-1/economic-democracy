Section III · Scale, Labor & the Machine

A. Philip Randolph

Labor Rights, Racial Justice, and Economic Citizenship

Suggested Quadrant: I 1889–1979 Labor & Civil Rights Leader

To understand A. Philip Randolph, you have to begin with a structural question: what does it mean to be fully included in an economy?

In the early to mid-20th century, Black workers in the United States faced exclusion not only from political rights but from stable employment, unions, and economic opportunity. Access to jobs—especially in key industries—was often restricted by race.

Randolph’s work addressed this exclusion directly.

At the center of his worldview is a defining claim:

Economic justice is a prerequisite for full citizenship.

As the founder of the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters, Randolph organized Black railway workers into one of the first successful Black-led labor unions. This was not only a labor victory—it was a breakthrough in economic inclusion, demonstrating that marginalized workers could build collective power within the economy.

From this perspective, labor rights and civil rights are inseparable.

Randolph understood that access to employment, fair wages, and workplace protections were essential to achieving broader equality. Without economic stability, political rights remain limited in practice.

He also emphasized the strategic use of pressure.

Randolph famously threatened a March on Washington in 1941 to protest discrimination in defense industries. The pressure led President Roosevelt to issue Executive Order 8802, banning racial discrimination in defense employment and establishing the Fair Employment Practices Committee.

This demonstrated a key mechanism:

Organized pressure can produce institutional change.

Randolph later helped shape the 1963 March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom, reinforcing the connection between economic opportunity and civil rights.

Perspective Supporters

Supporters see Randolph as a foundational figure in linking labor and racial justice.

They argue that he expanded the scope of civil rights to include economic inclusion and demonstrated how organized labor could serve as a vehicle for broader social change.

From this perspective, Randolph expands the analysis of economic systems to include who is allowed to participate—and under what conditions.

Perspective Critics

Critics, where they exist, tend to focus on the broader challenges of labor organizing and policy implementation rather than Randolph’s core analysis.

Questions remain about how to sustain labor power across different sectors and economic contexts.

A deeper tension lies in the relationship between access and power.

Is inclusion in existing systems sufficient, or must those systems be transformed? And how can economic rights be secured in ways that are durable across political and economic cycles?

A. Philip Randolph did not separate economic and political life. He understood them as mutually reinforcing—arguing that true citizenship requires both rights and resources.

His legacy raises enduring questions: What does it mean to be fully included in an economy? How can labor organizing advance broader social justice goals? And what institutions are necessary to ensure that economic opportunity is not limited by race or identity?
