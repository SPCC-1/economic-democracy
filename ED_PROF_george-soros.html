Section V · Money, Wealth & Who Controls It

George Soros

Reflexivity, Open Society, and the Power of Financial Actors

Suggested Quadrant: I / II 1930–present Investor & Philanthropist

To understand George Soros, you have to begin with a philosophical question: how do beliefs and perceptions shape economic reality?

Traditional economic theory assumes that markets reflect underlying fundamentals. Prices move toward equilibrium based on objective information.

Soros challenges that assumption.

At the center of his worldview is a defining claim:

Markets are not purely rational systems — they are shaped by feedback loops between perception and reality.

He calls this concept reflexivity. Investors’ beliefs influence market outcomes, and those outcomes, in turn, reinforce or alter beliefs. This creates self-reinforcing cycles — booms and busts driven not just by fundamentals, but by collective expectations.

From this perspective, markets can diverge from reality for extended periods. This creates a distinct implication: financial actors are not passive observers — they actively shape the systems they participate in.

Soros’s own career reflects this dynamic. As a hedge fund manager, he became known for identifying and acting on market imbalances, most famously during the 1992 currency crisis involving the British pound. His trades did not just anticipate outcomes — they contributed to them.

This reflects a broader framework:

Financial markets are inherently unstable and require oversight.

Soros argues that left unchecked, reflexive dynamics can lead to bubbles, crashes, and systemic crises. This introduces a role for regulation and policy to stabilize markets and mitigate excesses.

At the same time, his work extends beyond finance. Soros is a major proponent of the concept of the “open society,” influenced by philosopher Karl Popper. This vision emphasizes democratic governance, transparency, and the protection of individual freedoms against authoritarian control. This introduces a key linkage: economic systems and political systems are deeply interconnected.

Perspective Supporters

Supporters see Soros as both theorist and practitioner.

They argue that his concept of reflexivity provides a more realistic understanding of markets than purely rational models. His philanthropic work is viewed as an extension of his belief in open, democratic societies. From this perspective, Soros expands economics to include psychology, politics, and institutional design.

Perspective Critics

Critics, however, raise multiple concerns.

Some question the consistency between his market activities and his critiques of financial instability. Others argue that large financial actors, including Soros himself, can exert outsized influence on markets and politics. There are also broader debates about the role of philanthropy in shaping public life.

A deeper tension lies in power and perception. If markets are shaped by beliefs, then those who can influence beliefs — or act at scale — hold significant power. Soros’s work makes that explicit. He does not view markets as neutral mechanisms, but as dynamic systems influenced by human cognition, institutional structures, and political context.

George Soros reframes finance as a feedback system — demonstrating that understanding markets requires understanding how ideas, expectations, and actions interact to produce economic outcomes.

Are markets fundamentally rational or reflexive? What role should regulation play in stabilizing financial systems? And how should societies balance financial power with democratic accountability?
