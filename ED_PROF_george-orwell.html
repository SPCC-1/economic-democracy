Section VIII · Who Tells the Story of the Economy

George Orwell

Participation through Informed Awareness

Suggested Quadrant: I 1903–1950 Writer

To understand George Orwell, you have to understand power — and how language, information, and narrative shape what people are able to see, say, and ultimately do within a political and economic system.

Orwell wrote in response to the totalitarian regimes of the 20th century, where centralized control extended beyond economics into thought itself. Systems of power did not rely solely on coercion; they operated through propaganda, surveillance, and the manipulation of truth. The problem, as Orwell saw it, was not only inequality or inefficiency, but the erosion of reality as a shared foundation for democratic life.

His central claim is epistemic:

Control over information is control over power.

In works like 1984 and Animal Farm, Orwell explores how language can be used to obscure, distort, or redefine reality. When words lose meaning, accountability collapses. Economic and political systems can no longer be evaluated if the terms used to describe them are unstable or manipulated.

This leads to a broader framework:

Democracy depends on clarity of language and access to truth.

In Orwell's analysis, economic democracy is inseparable from informational integrity. Participation requires not only formal rights, but the ability to understand the system one is participating in. If information is controlled or distorted, agency becomes illusory.

He also examines the dynamics of power concentration. Even systems that begin with egalitarian intentions can evolve into hierarchies if power is not continuously scrutinized. Animal Farm illustrates how revolutionary movements can reproduce the very inequalities they sought to eliminate, often through subtle shifts in language and governance.

This reflects a distinct model: participation through informed awareness. Economic agency, in this framework, depends on the capacity to perceive and question power structures. Transparency, free expression, and independent inquiry become essential components of any democratic system.

Perspective Supporters

Supporters see Orwell as a critical guardian of democratic integrity.

They argue that his work provides tools for identifying manipulation, propaganda, and the concentration of power. In an era of mass media and digital information, his insights are often applied to questions of misinformation, narrative control, and institutional trust. From this perspective, economic democracy requires not only fair systems, but truthful ones.

Perspective Critics

Critics, however, note limitations in scope.

Orwell's work focuses heavily on political and informational power, with less direct engagement in economic structures or policy design. While his critiques illuminate risks, they do not provide a detailed framework for constructing alternative economic systems.

A deeper critique examines application. How can societies maintain informational integrity in complex, decentralized media environments? Who determines what is true, and how is that authority established or contested? And how do economic incentives within media systems shape the production and distribution of information?

George Orwell did not propose an economic model. He diagnosed a vulnerability. His work highlights that without a shared commitment to truth, no system — economic or political — can remain accountable.

His legacy raises enduring questions: How is power maintained through language? What safeguards protect against manipulation? And can democratic systems function when information itself becomes contested terrain?

These questions remain unresolved.
