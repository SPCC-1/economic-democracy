Section VII · Economic Anger, Identity & Mobilization

Jesse Jackson

Coalition Politics, Economic Inclusion, and the Expansion of Democracy

Suggested Quadrant: I 1941–present Minister & Civil Rights Leader

To understand Jesse Jackson, you have to begin with a political question: how do marginalized groups build enough power to influence national policy and economic outcomes?

Jackson, a civil rights leader and founder of the Rainbow/PUSH Coalition, developed a framework centered on coalition building, economic inclusion, and expanded democratic participation. His work extends the civil rights movement into the arenas of electoral politics, corporate accountability, and global economic justice.

At the center of his worldview is a defining claim:

Political power must be broadened to include those historically excluded from economic opportunity.

Jackson argued that racial minorities, working-class communities, farmers, and other marginalized groups shared overlapping economic interests. By organizing these groups into a unified political force — the “Rainbow Coalition” — he sought to translate social movements into electoral and economic influence.

This creates a distinct analytical focus: the relationship between coalition building and economic power.

Jackson also emphasized economic justice as a core component of civil rights. He advocated for policies such as fair employment, minority business development, and equitable access to capital, arguing that political rights must be matched by economic opportunity.

This introduces a key dynamic:

Political inclusion versus economic exclusion.

Through initiatives like Operation PUSH, Jackson worked to pressure corporations to diversify hiring, contracting, and investment practices. He viewed corporate behavior as a critical lever in shaping economic outcomes. Private institutions, in this framework, play a central role in public equity.

Jackson's presidential campaigns in the 1980s expanded the scope of national political debate, bringing issues such as inequality, labor rights, and international justice into mainstream discourse. Elections, in his view, served as platforms for movement-building.

Perspective Supporters

Supporters view Jackson as a key figure in translating civil rights activism into political and economic strategy.

His work is seen as expanding participation and advocating for systemic inclusion. By building coalitions across racial, economic, and geographic lines, Jackson demonstrated that shared interests could be organized into lasting political power.

Perspective Critics

Critics argue that coalition-based politics can be difficult to sustain, particularly when groups have differing priorities.

Others question the effectiveness of corporate pressure campaigns in producing long-term structural change. This introduces a familiar tension: coalition breadth versus strategic focus. A deeper question lies in durability — how can diverse coalitions maintain unity while advancing concrete economic goals?

Jackson's work does not fully resolve this. Instead, it tests the model. He represents a continuation and expansion of civil rights into political economy — one that centers coalition building, economic inclusion, and democratic participation.

How do marginalized groups build lasting political power? What strategies link civil rights to economic outcomes? And how can coalitions translate shared interests into sustained change?
