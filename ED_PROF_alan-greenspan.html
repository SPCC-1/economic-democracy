Section V · Money, Wealth & Who Controls It

Alan Greenspan

Participation through Stable Monetary Conditions

Suggested Quadrant: III / IV 1926–present Chair of the Federal Reserve

To understand Alan Greenspan, you have to understand stewardship—and the attempt to guide a complex, market-driven economy through monetary policy rather than direct control.

Greenspan led the Federal Reserve during a period of expanding globalization, financial innovation, and relatively stable growth. Inflation had been tamed, markets were deepening, and new financial instruments were reshaping capital flows. The problem was no longer simply crisis response, but ongoing calibration: how to maintain stability in an increasingly dynamic and interconnected system.

His central premise is technocratic:

Macroeconomic stability can be managed through disciplined monetary policy.

At the core of Greenspan’s approach was the use of interest rates to influence economic behavior—cooling inflation when the economy overheated, and stimulating growth during downturns. Rather than directing investment or production, the Federal Reserve shaped the conditions under which markets operated.

This reflects a broader framework:

Markets function best when guided, not controlled.

Greenspan believed that market participants, driven by incentives and information, were generally better at allocating resources than centralized authorities. The role of the central bank was to maintain price stability and respond to systemic risks, not to interfere with market mechanisms unnecessarily.

He also expressed confidence in financial innovation. Deregulation and the expansion of complex financial products were seen as ways to distribute risk more efficiently across the system. In this view, markets evolve to become more resilient as they develop new tools and structures.

Economic democracy, in this framework, is indirect. Individuals and institutions participate in markets that are shaped by macroeconomic policy. Stability—low inflation, predictable interest rates—creates an environment where investment, employment, and growth can occur.

Perspective Supporters

Supporters see Greenspan as a steady steward of the economy.

They argue that his tenure was marked by sustained growth, low inflation, and the avoidance of major crises for much of his leadership. By allowing markets to function while intervening selectively, Greenspan is credited with maintaining a balance between flexibility and stability.

Perspective Critics

Critics, however, focus on systemic risks that emerged under his watch.

They argue that an overreliance on market self-regulation contributed to the buildup of financial imbalances leading to the 2008 crisis. Deregulation and the expansion of complex financial instruments, while innovative, also increased opacity and interconnected risk. Critics also question whether monetary policy alone is sufficient to manage inequality and structural imbalances.

A deeper critique examines the limits of technocracy. If central banks wield significant influence over economic conditions, how are they held accountable? To what extent should unelected institutions shape economic outcomes? And can monetary policy address problems that are fundamentally structural rather than cyclical?

Alan Greenspan did not design the market system, but he played a central role in managing its conditions during a critical period of expansion.

His legacy raises enduring questions: What is the proper role of central banks? How much should markets be trusted to self-regulate? And what are the risks of relying on monetary policy as the primary tool of economic governance?

These questions remain contested.
