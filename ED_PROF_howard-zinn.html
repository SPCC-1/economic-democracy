Section VII · Economic Anger, Identity & Mobilization

Howard Zinn

History, Power, and the Voices from Below

Suggested Quadrant: I 1922–2010 Historian & Activist

To understand Howard Zinn, you have to begin with a historiographical question: who gets to tell the story of a nation?

Zinn, a historian and political thinker, challenged traditional narratives of American history by shifting the focus away from political elites and toward ordinary people—workers, women, Indigenous communities, and those historically excluded from power.

At the center of his worldview is a defining claim:

History is not neutral—it reflects the perspective of those who write it.

In A People’s History of the United States, Zinn reconstructs American history through the experiences of those who resisted exploitation and injustice. He emphasizes that progress has often come not from leaders alone, but from collective action and social movements. From this perspective, history is contested terrain.

Zinn argues that dominant narratives tend to legitimize existing institutions by highlighting moments of unity, progress, and leadership, while minimizing conflict, inequality, and dissent. By contrast, his work foregrounds struggle—labor strikes, civil rights movements, anti-war protests—as central drivers of change.

Economically, Zinn critiques systems that concentrate wealth and power, linking historical developments—industrialization, expansion, and globalization—to patterns of inequality and exploitation. He situates economic structures within broader political and social dynamics.

This reflects a broader framework:

Economic systems shape, and are shaped by, historical conflict.

Zinn’s work is also explicitly normative. He does not claim detachment, but rather argues that scholars and citizens alike have a responsibility to engage with questions of justice and equity. This expands the conversation into the tension between objectivity and moral responsibility.

Perspective Supporters

Supporters view Zinn as a transformative figure in historical scholarship and education.

His work is seen as democratizing history—making visible the contributions and struggles of those often left out of conventional accounts. By centering power, conflict, and the lived experiences of ordinary people, Zinn reoriented historical analysis toward the questions that matter most to democratic life.

Perspective Critics

Critics argue that his narrative can be selective, emphasizing conflict and injustice at the expense of institutional achievements or complexity.

They contend that his framework risks substituting one partial narrative for another. This introduces a familiar tension: revision versus balance.

A deeper question lies in interpretation. If all history is shaped by perspective, how should societies construct shared narratives? And what role should those narratives play in shaping present-day policy and identity? Zinn’s work does not resolve these tensions. Instead, it reframes them.

Howard Zinn represents a reorientation of historical analysis: one that centers power, conflict, and the lived experiences of ordinary people.

Who tells the story of a nation? What voices are missing from that story? And how does our understanding of the past shape the choices we make about the future?
