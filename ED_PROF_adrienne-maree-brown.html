Section IV · The Digital Revolution & Its Critics

adrienne maree brown

Emergence, Adaptation, and the Practice of Collective Change

Suggested Quadrant: I 1978–present Writer & Facilitator

To understand adrienne maree brown, you have to begin with a systems question: how do complex social systems change?

In many models of change, transformation is imagined as top-down — driven by policy, institutions, or centralized leadership. Brown offers a different lens.

At the center of her worldview is a defining claim:

Sustainable change emerges from small, interconnected actions that scale through relationships.

Drawing from complexity science, social movements, and Black feminist thought, she emphasizes “emergence” — the idea that large-scale patterns arise from local interactions. Change is not imposed; it evolves.

From this perspective, scale is relational.

Systems shift when individuals and communities build new patterns of behavior, trust, and cooperation. These patterns can spread, adapt, and grow over time.

This creates a different form of power:

Distributed agency across networks of people.

Rather than concentrating control, brown’s framework focuses on participation. Each person contributes to the system, and collective outcomes emerge from these contributions.

She also emphasizes adaptation.

Rigid systems struggle to respond to changing conditions. By contrast, adaptive systems — those that learn, adjust, and evolve — are more resilient.

This reflects a broader framework:

Social transformation is a living process, not a fixed plan.

Perspective Supporters

Supporters see brown as a guide to movement-building.

They argue that her work provides practical tools for organizing, collaboration, and long-term change. By focusing on relationships, care, and iterative learning, she offers an alternative to purely hierarchical models.

From this perspective, brown expands the analysis of economic and social systems to include emergence, interdependence, and collective practice.

Perspective Critics

Critics, however, raise questions about scale and structure.

They argue that decentralized approaches may struggle to address large, entrenched systems that require coordinated action. Without clear leadership or institutional leverage, change may be slow or uneven.

There are also debates about how emergent strategies interact with formal power structures.

A deeper tension lies in the relationship between flexibility and coordination. How can systems remain adaptive while achieving large-scale outcomes? What forms of organization are needed to connect local action with broader change?

Brown’s work emphasizes imagination and practice. She encourages communities to experiment, learn from failure, and build systems that reflect shared values.

adrienne maree brown does not operate primarily through formal institutions. But she offers a framework for how systems evolve — demonstrating that transformation can emerge from the collective actions of many.

Her legacy raises enduring questions: How do small actions scale into systemic change? What role do relationships and trust play in transformation? And how can societies balance decentralized emergence with the need for coordinated action?
