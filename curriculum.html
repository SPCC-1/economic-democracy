The Argument

Public debates about the economy often sound chaotic, polarized, or emotional. People argue past one another using the same words — freedom, innovation, fairness, democracy — but mean very different things by them. Most political frameworks rely on a single left-right spectrum. That approach fails to capture the complexity of modern economic life, especially in an era of global markets, digital platforms, and artificial intelligence.

This curriculum uses two independent axes to map economic worldviews: where power is located, and how accountable it should be to democracy. Together, these axes reveal four distinct ways of thinking about the economy. None of them is simply correct. Each reflects different values, different assessments of risk, and different readings of history.

The goal is not consensus. It is democratic reasoning — the capacity to identify assumptions, distinguish values from facts, understand tradeoffs, and revise one's position in light of evidence. These are skills that matter beyond any economics class.

Quadrant I Public Power, Democratic Accountability Markets should be shaped to serve collective goals. Regulation is a feature, not a bug.
Quadrant II Private Markets, Democratic Guardrails Private enterprise drives progress, but requires democratic limits to prevent abuse.
Quadrant III Public Power, Expert Control Complex systems require coordinated expertise. Democracy matters, but often indirectly.
Quadrant IV Private Power, Elite Control Progress comes from builders, not voters. Speed and scale are advantages, not threats.
